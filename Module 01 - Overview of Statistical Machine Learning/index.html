What Is Machine Learning

A working definition: machine learning is concerned with the design and development of algorithms that allow computers to improve their performance over time, based on data.

The improvement is something we can observe and measure directly, performance on some task gets better over time, and it comes from data rather than from new insight by the person who wrote the algorithm.

Key shift: the algorithm itself stays fixed. What changes is the model's parameters, learned from data, not the code a programmer writes by hand.

The Formal Definition

The most widely cited formal definition comes from Tom Mitchell, 1998:

Definition. A computer program is said to learn from experience E with respect to some class of tasks T and a performance measure P, if its performance at tasks in T, as measured by P, improves with experience E.

Worked example, spam filtering

ComponentWhat it is here
Task, TClassify an incoming email as spam or not spam
Performance measure, PPercentage of emails correctly classified
Experience, EA collection of emails that a user has already labeled as spam or not spam

Motivating Examples, Around the World

Statistical machine learning is not an abstract exercise, it already runs quietly behind many everyday systems.

Email and text

  • Spam filtering, learning what a user considers junk mail
  • Priority inbox, learning what a user considers important

Vision

  • Handwritten digit recognition, ZIP codes, cheque amounts
  • Galaxy classification from sky survey images

Recommendation and ranking

  • Streaming and shopping apps suggesting what to watch or buy next, based on past behavior
  • Social feeds ranking which posts a user is likely to engage with

Signals

  • Image denoising, learning typical patterns in natural images
  • Separating mixed audio sources, the cocktail party problem

Medical Diagnosis and Risk Prediction

One of the most consequential applications of statistical learning is medical decision support, learning from past patient records and outcomes to help flag risk earlier.

  • Predicting risk of disease from clinical measurements and history
  • Flagging abnormal scans or images for closer review by a clinician
  • Estimating how a patient is likely to respond to a treatment
Important framing: in nearly all of these systems, the model supports a human decision maker, it does not replace one. We will return to this distinction, prediction versus inference, in a few slides.

A South Asia Anchored Example

Two examples close to home, in Bangladesh and the wider South Asia region.

Mobile financial service fraud detection

Services such as bKash and Nagad process enormous volumes of small transactions every day. A model can learn the typical pattern of a genuine transaction, amount, time of day, location, and frequency, and flag transactions that deviate sharply from that pattern for review.

Flood risk prediction

Bangladesh experiences seasonal flooding driven by rainfall, river levels, and upstream conditions. A statistical model trained on historical rainfall, river gauge readings, and past flood extents can help estimate the risk of flooding in a district over the coming days, supporting early warning systems.

Pattern to notice: in both examples, the model is given historical data with some notion of the outcome we care about, fraud or not, flood or not, and it learns a rule that generalizes to new, unseen situations.

What Is Common to All These Examples

Strip away the surface differences, spam, digits, flooding, fraud, and a shared structure appears.

  1. We are given some data, for example, past transactions, past rainfall readings, or labeled images
  2. We are sometimes given extra information about that data, for example, which transactions were later confirmed as fraud
  3. The goal is to build a system that learns a useful rule from the given data, using that extra information when it is available
This is the pattern we will formalize next: data, sometimes labels, and a rule we want to learn, written using the language of statistics.

A Short History

1960s
Symbolic AI. Computers learn rules from data, but the underlying statistics is rarely analyzed formally.
1980s
Artificial neural networks gain attention as a flexible class of models.
1990s to 2000s
Statistical machine learning matures, kernel methods, decision trees, graphical models, with explicit statistical justification.
2010s onward
Deep learning combined with large datasets and fast hardware drives breakthroughs in image recognition, speech recognition, and language translation.

Why statistical machine learning took off when it did

  • Faster computers with enough memory to represent statistical models
  • Mature numerical libraries for linear algebra and optimization
  • Large amounts of data becoming available to learn from

Supervised Learning

In supervised learning, we are given pairs of data and targets, \( (x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n) \), and the goal is to learn a mapping from \( x \) to \( y \) that generalizes well to new, unseen data.

  • \( x_i \), the input, also called a feature, predictor, or example
  • \( y_i \), the target, also called a label, response, or outcome
  • The learned mapping is sometimes written \( f \), so that \( y \approx f(x) \)
Example: in the flood risk problem, \( x \) might be rainfall and river level readings for a district, and \( y \) is whether flooding occurred, the label we are trying to predict for new districts and new weeks.

Unsupervised Learning

In unsupervised learning, we are given only the data \( x_1, \ldots, x_n \), with no target \( y \) at all. The goal is to discover interesting structure already present in the data.

  • Grouping similar observations together, clustering
  • Finding a small number of underlying patterns that explain most of the variation in the data
Example: a mobile operator groups customers into segments based on call and data usage patterns, with no predefined labels for what each segment should be. The structure comes entirely from the data itself.

Supervised vs Unsupervised

Supervised learning

  • Data comes with known targets, \( y \)
  • Goal: predict \( y \) for new \( x \)
  • Examples: spam or not spam, flood or no flood
  • Success is measurable directly, compare prediction to the true label

Unsupervised learning

  • Data has no targets at all
  • Goal: discover structure in \( x \) alone
  • Examples: customer segmentation, finding patterns in gene expression data
  • Success is harder to measure, there is no single right answer to compare against

Most of this course focuses on supervised learning, since it is where the comparison between prediction and truth is most direct, but unsupervised methods appear later, in the module on unsupervised learning.

Reinforcement Learning

Reinforcement learning sits apart from both of the above. An agent takes actions in an environment and receives a reward, sometimes only after a long sequence of actions, sometimes only at the very end.

  • No correct input and output pairs are ever presented directly
  • The reward for an action may only become clear much later
  • The agent must balance exploration, trying new actions, against exploitation, using actions already known to work well
Example: a ride hailing app deciding which driver to dispatch to which rider can be framed as reinforcement learning. Each dispatch decision is an action, and the reward, a completed trip, a good rating, a cancellation, only becomes clear afterward, yet the app must learn over time which dispatch decisions tend to work out well.

Reinforcement learning is mentioned here for completeness. It is not a focus of this course.

Other Learning Types, Briefly

A few other learning settings appear in the wider literature, mentioned here so the taxonomy is complete.

  • Active learning, the algorithm itself chooses which new example to request a label for next, rather than passively receiving a fixed dataset
  • Transduction, the algorithm is allowed to see the unlabeled test inputs, but never their labels, while it is building the model
  • Semi-supervised learning, a small amount of labeled data is combined with a much larger amount of unlabeled data

We will not use these settings directly in this course, but recognizing the names is useful when reading further machine learning material.

Batch Learning vs Online Learning

Beyond the type of learning problem, there is also a question of when the data arrives.

Batch learning

  • All training data is available up front
  • Learn one mapping from the entire training set
  • Apply the fixed, learned mapping to new data later

Online learning

  • Data arrives one example, or one small batch, at a time
  • The model is updated and refined after each new example
  • Useful when data is continuously streaming, such as live transactions

This course works almost entirely in the batch setting, a fixed training dataset, since that is the typical setting for the statistical methods we will study.

Basic Notation, Data

We will reuse a small amount of notation throughout the course, so it is worth fixing it now.

A single observation with \( D \) input variables is written as a vector \( x = (x_1, \ldots, x_D)^{T} \). With \( N \) observations, we collect them into a data matrix \( X \), with one row per observation.

Plain meaning: if you think of a spreadsheet of data, each row of the data matrix \( X \) is one observation, for example, one customer or one district, and each column is one measured variable, for example, age or rainfall.

Basic Notation, Targets

For supervised learning, each observation also has a target. We collect the \( N \) targets into a vector \( y = (y_1, \ldots, y_N)^{T} \).

  • A target can be quantitative, a real number, such as rainfall in millimeters
  • A target can be qualitative, one value from a finite set of categories, such as flood or no flood
Why this distinction matters: it determines whether the problem is a regression problem, quantitative target, or a classification problem, qualitative target. We expand on this in the next module.

Why Estimate f

In supervised learning we often write the relationship between inputs and the target as

\[ Y = f(X) + \varepsilon \]

where \( f \) is some fixed but unknown function, and \( \varepsilon \) is random error that does not depend on \( X \). There are two distinct reasons we might want to estimate \( f \).

Prediction

We care about the predicted value \( \hat{y} \) for a new input \( x \). We do not need to fully understand the exact form of \( f \), as long as predictions are accurate.

Inference

We care about understanding how \( Y \) changes as the inputs change, which inputs matter, and how. The form of \( f \) itself is the object of interest.

Prediction vs Inference, Contrasted

Question askedPrediction or inference
Will this specific transaction turn out to be fraudulentPrediction
Which features of a transaction are most associated with fraudInference
What is the expected rainfall in a district next weekPrediction
How does upstream river level causally affect downstream flood riskInference
A common confusion: a model can be excellent for prediction while being nearly impossible to interpret, and a model can be very interpretable while predicting poorly. The two goals do not always align, and most modules in this course will note which goal a method is best suited for.

The Data Science Workflow

Whether the eventual goal is prediction or inference, most statistical learning projects follow a similar overall workflow.

  1. Collect the data relevant to the question
  2. Explore the data, look at summaries, distributions, and obvious problems such as missing values
  3. Model the relationship between inputs and the target using an appropriate method
  4. Validate the model on data it has not seen before
  5. Communicate the results to the people who will use them
Notice the loop: in practice, this workflow is rarely a straight line. Exploring the data often sends you back to collect more, and validating a model often sends you back to revise it. Expect to circle back.

Quantitative vs Qualitative Variables

Variables, both inputs and targets, come in two broad flavors, and this distinction shapes which methods apply.

Quantitative

  • Numeric, takes values on a continuous or countable scale
  • Examples: rainfall in millimeters, transaction amount, a patient's blood pressure

Qualitative

  • Categorical, takes one of a finite set of values
  • Examples: flood or no flood, spam or not spam, blood type
Rule of thumb: a quantitative target gives a regression problem, a qualitative target gives a classification problem. We dedicate full modules to each later in the course.

Model Flexibility vs Interpretability

Statistical learning methods range from highly restrictive, and easy to interpret, to highly flexible, and harder to interpret.

  • Restrictive methods, such as linear regression, produce a small number of coefficients that are easy to explain, at the cost of being able to capture only simple relationships
  • Flexible methods, such as advanced tree based ensembles, can capture complicated, irregular relationships, but the resulting model is much harder to explain to a human reader
Choosing a point on the spectrum: when the goal is inference, explaining which factors matter, a more restrictive, interpretable method is often preferred. When the goal is pure prediction accuracy, a more flexible method is often preferred.

The Bias Variance Idea, Conceptually

A model that is too simple may consistently miss real patterns in the data, this is called bias. A model that is too flexible may fit the training data almost perfectly, including its noise, and then perform poorly on new data, this is called variance.

  • High bias, low variance: a very simple model, stable but systematically wrong
  • Low bias, high variance: a very flexible model, accurate on training data but unstable on new data
Why we stop here: the formal bias variance decomposition, with its exact mathematical formula, is introduced properly in Module 2, alongside the tools to measure it. For now, hold onto the intuition, very simple models can underfit, very flexible models can overfit.

The Strategy We Will Follow

Across this course, most supervised learning topics follow a consistent strategy.

  1. Identify the type of input \( x \) and target \( y \), quantitative or qualitative
  2. Propose a mathematical model for the relationship between \( x \) and \( y \)
  3. Define an objective, an error or likelihood, that measures how well the model fits the data
  4. Find the model parameters that optimize that objective
  5. Quantify uncertainty around the fitted model and its predictions
  6. Interpret and diagnose the results, checking whether the model is reasonable

Keep this six step pattern in mind. You will see it again, applied to a new method, in nearly every module of this course.

Machine Learning and Its Related Fields

Statistical machine learning did not appear in isolation, it draws on, and contributes to, several neighboring fields.

Statistics

Descriptive statistics summarizes data, inferential statistics draws conclusions about a process while accounting for uncertainty. Statistical learning borrows both ideas directly.

Artificial intelligence

A broader field concerned with reasoning, planning, perception, and learning. Machine learning is one major approach within artificial intelligence.

Data mining

Searching very large databases, for example years of retail transactions or call records, for hidden patterns. It shares many techniques with unsupervised learning, but often emphasizes scale over formal statistical guarantees.

Computer science

Provides the algorithms, data structures, and computational complexity analysis that make large scale statistical learning computationally feasible.

Putting It Together, A Worked Walkthrough

Consider the flood risk example one more time, now tracing it through every idea from this module.

  1. Type of learning: supervised, since past data includes whether flooding actually occurred
  2. Type of target: qualitative, flood or no flood, so this is a classification problem
  3. Goal: mainly prediction, we want an accurate early warning for new weeks and new districts, though inference, which factors matter most, is also useful to planners
  4. Workflow: collect rainfall and river level history, explore it for gaps and outliers, fit a model, validate it on weeks the model has not seen, communicate the risk level to disaster management authorities
  5. Tradeoff: a simple, interpretable model may be preferred if officials need to understand why a warning was issued, a more flexible model may be preferred if raw predictive accuracy is the only goal
This is the pattern: every module from here on will ask you to make exactly these kinds of decisions, type of target, prediction or inference, how flexible a model to choose, for a new method.
